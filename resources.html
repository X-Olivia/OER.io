Publicly accessible datasets

Dataset Type Scale Why it matters
mcPHASES Wearable + hormonal + self-report 42 participants across two study periods One of the clearest open datasets for connecting wearables with menstrual-health labels and hormone measures.
Menstrual Cycle Data (Marquette) Natural family planning records Historical human-subject dataset Useful for cycle structure, mucus, fertility-awareness, and non-wearable historical modelling.
Kaggle re-hosts and app-style exports Mostly self-reported tabular data Varies by upload Convenient for quick experiments, but provenance and preprocessing quality require extra checking. A local analysis page and experiment summary are available on this site.

Public access does not mean uniform quality. Always record the label source, missing-data pattern, cohort filters, and whether the dataset reflects real deployment conditions or a more controlled study.

Why most influential datasets are not fully open

Large studies based on menstrual-health apps and consumer devices have shaped the field, but the raw datasets behind many of those papers are usually not released openly. Inference from the literature: this is likely due to a mix of privacy constraints, commercial ownership, consent scope, and the high sensitivity of reproductive-health data.

  • App-based cohort papers demonstrate scale, but not necessarily public reuse access [1] [2] [3].
  • Wearable-device studies often release findings rather than longitudinal raw data streams [4] [5].
  • Open resources such as mcPHASES are therefore especially valuable because they allow method comparison and pipeline debugging [6].

A realistic starter path for new researchers

Option A: strong baseline work

Use self-reported or cycle-history data, build transparent calendar and history baselines, and make the evaluation protocol unusually clean. This is often more publishable than a weak wearable model on noisy labels.

Option B: wearable proof of concept

Use mcPHASES or another well-described dataset to test temperature and cardiovascular features, but define the endpoint carefully and avoid overclaiming generalisability from a small cohort.

Option C: clinically anchored small study

If you can collect your own data, a modest but well-labelled cohort with explicit hormone or LH anchoring may be more scientifically useful than a larger weakly labelled convenience sample.

Option D: evaluation-method paper

A paper on subgrouping irregularity, standardising metrics, or comparing label definitions can be a legitimate contribution because these issues currently block meaningful comparison.

Reproducibility checklist

  1. State the prediction target explicitly: next bleed, ovulation day, fertile window, or phase.
  2. State the label rule explicitly: for example LH onset, LH peak, or peak + 1 day.
  3. Describe what information is available at prediction time and what is forbidden.
  4. Prefer subject-wise splits when claiming generalisation to unseen people.
  5. Report both continuous error and window-based accuracy where appropriate.
  6. Describe missingness and adherence rather than silently dropping hard cases.
  7. State whether the cohort is regular-only, mixed, or explicitly irregular.
