Orientation for new researchers

The field mixes several related tasks: predicting the next bleed, estimating ovulation timing, classifying cycle phase, and estimating the fertile window. These tasks share biology, but they do not share identical labels, metrics, or deployment constraints.

Target Typical label source Common output Main difficulty
Next menstruation onset Self-tracked bleeding logs Next date or days-until-onset Strongly affected by variable follicular timing and logging quality
Ovulation day Urinary LH, ultrasound, progesterone, or proxy rules Single day estimate or error window Operational definitions differ substantially across papers
Fertile window Derived from ovulation estimate or fertility-awareness rules Binary daily probability or start/end window Often clinically sensitive and definition-dependent
Cycle phase Calendar rules, hormone assays, or latent-state labels Daily phase class Phase boundaries may be operational rather than direct biological truth

Menstrual cycle basics that matter for modelling

Clinical guidance and large observational datasets both reject the idea of a universally fixed 28-day cycle. For modelling, the important point is not only that the cycle is variable, but that the variability is structured: ovulation is a short event, the follicular phase is often the main source of timing variability, and the luteal phase is usually more stable than the pre-ovulatory portion of the cycle [1] [2] [3].

Phase What happens Relative stability Why modellers care
Menstrual phase Bleeding marks day 1 of a new cycle Observable, but self-tracking can be noisy Often the easiest event to collect at scale
Follicular phase Follicle recruitment and hormone-driven preparation before ovulation Usually the most variable segment Much of onset-prediction error originates here
Ovulation Release of the oocyte, often anchored with LH-based testing Short event and difficult to observe directly Useful anchor for downstream countdown-style models
Luteal phase Post-ovulatory progesterone-dominant phase Relatively more stable Explains why predicting menses after ovulation is often easier
  • Normal variation is common; perfect regularity is not the right default assumption [4] [5].
  • Ovulation does not reliably happen at cycle midpoint, so midpoint counting is structurally weak [6].
  • Irregularity is heterogeneous: long cycles, short but stable cycles, and high-variability cycles may need different modelling assumptions.

Glossary of signals and menstrual-prediction terms

Term Type Meaning and why it matters
BBT (Basal body temperature) Temperature The lowest body temperature at rest, traditionally measured immediately after waking. It remains a classic reference for biphasic cycle patterns.
Core body temperature Temperature Internal temperature closer to true thermoregulation. Harder to measure continuously in daily life than skin-based proxies.
WST (Wrist skin temperature) Wearable temperature Nightly or continuous temperature captured at the wrist. Useful at scale, but influenced by ambient conditions and device placement [7].
Finger / distal skin temperature Wearable temperature Nocturnal peripheral temperature, often measured by rings. Can show clear cycle-related shifts in free-living data [8].
Peripheral body temperature Wearable temperature A broader label for skin-surface temperature proxies used by some consumer devices; not interchangeable with BBT or core temperature.
HR (Heart rate) Cardiovascular Beats per minute. Many studies use resting, nocturnal, or sleeping HR rather than daytime HR because activity introduces noise [9].
RHR / sleeping HR Cardiovascular Heart rate during rest or sleep. Often more stable than daytime HR and increasingly used for phase classification and ovulation detection [10].
IBI (Inter-beat interval) Cardiovascular Time interval between beats. HRV metrics are derived from it.
HRV (Heart rate variability) Autonomic Beat-to-beat variability, often summarised as RMSSD or frequency-domain measures. Used as a proxy of autonomic regulation across the cycle.
RR (Respiratory rate) Respiratory Breaths per minute. Some wearables estimate it during sleep and use it as a complementary cycle signal.
EDA (Electrodermal activity) Autonomic Skin conductance related to sympathetic arousal. Appears in some multimodal passive-sensing studies, but is far less standard than temperature or HR.
Skin perfusion / blood flow Peripheral physiology Changes in vascular tone can contribute to skin-temperature and pulse patterns, especially in distal body sites.
Sleep timing and duration Behavioral context Often used as covariates or indirect signals because sleep pattern changes can co-vary with the cycle and with signal quality.
Activity / steps Behavioral context Useful for contextualising daytime physiology and identifying high-noise periods.
LH (Luteinizing hormone) Hormonal biomarker Urinary LH surges shortly before ovulation. Often treated as the pragmatic reference event for ovulation-focused wearable studies.
OPK (Ovulation predictor kit) Hormonal test Consumer urine test strip used to detect LH surge. Important because many "wearable" studies still depend on OPKs for labels.
E2 / estradiol Hormonal biomarker A key ovarian hormone driving follicular development. Useful for clinical interpretation and sometimes for hybrid predictive models.
E3G (Estrone-3-glucuronide) Urinary hormone metabolite A urinary estrogen metabolite used in fertility-monitoring systems. More common in fertility-awareness datasets than in consumer wearables.
Progesterone Hormonal biomarker Post-ovulatory hormone strongly linked to the thermal shift and luteal-phase physiology.
PdG (Pregnanediol glucuronide) Urinary hormone metabolite A urinary metabolite of progesterone used to retrospectively support ovulation confirmation.
FSH (Follicle-stimulating hormone) Hormonal biomarker Important for cycle physiology and some clinical workups, but less commonly available in real-world prediction datasets.
Anovulatory cycle Cycle type A cycle without ovulation. This matters because ovulation-anchored assumptions may fail entirely in such cycles.
Fertile window Task concept The days during which intercourse can lead to conception; often defined relative to ovulation rather than directly observed.
Subject-wise split Evaluation design Train and test users are disjoint. This is the safer evaluation protocol when claiming generalisation to unseen people.
Prospective / causal prediction Deployment constraint Prediction at day t uses only information available up to day t; no future observations from the current cycle may leak in.

Mathematical task definitions

The equations below are rendered as HTML, not Markdown. They are intentionally simple because the point here is to make the task framing explicit before discussing model architecture.

Notation

x1:tObserved signals up to day t
hUser history from earlier cycles
mNext menstruation onset
oOvulation day
ytPhase label for day t

Deployment rule

Prediction at day t may use only x1:t and h

This is the practical daily-prediction setting. Retrospective full-cycle estimation is a different and easier problem.

1. Next-menses onset prediction

m̂t = f(x1:t, h)

The model outputs either the next start date or the remaining days until next onset. MAE in days is common, but coverage within a fixed window should also be reported.

2. Daily phase classification

ŷt ∈ {menstrual, follicular, ovulatory, luteal}

This is a day-level state-estimation problem. Different papers use different phase granularity, so labels are not always directly comparable.

3. Ovulation-day estimation

ô = arg maxτ ≤ t P(Oτ = 1 | x1:t, h)

In a prospective setting the estimate can only rely on observed data so far. In a retrospective setting the full cycle may be available, which usually yields better numbers.

4. Fertile-window detection

P(FWt = 1 | x1:t, h)

This is usually derived from ovulation probability or fertility-awareness rules, so it inherits label uncertainty from upstream ovulation definitions.

Method families

Family Inputs Typical methods Strengths Weaknesses
Calendar / history-based Past bleeding dates and cycle statistics Forward counting, moving averages, Bayesian priors, hidden-state models Scalable, cheap, widely deployable Weak when history is short or cycles are unstable
Wearable physiology Continuous or nightly sensor streams Feature engineering, random forests, boosting, sequence models, signal-processing rules Can exploit physiological change rather than dates alone High missingness, device heterogeneity, small labelled cohorts
Clinical / biomarker-driven LH tests, ultrasound, blood draws, progesterone, estrogen markers Rule-based anchoring, fertility monitoring, diagnostic assessment Stronger biological reference High burden and less suited to low-friction deployment

Wearable signal taxonomy

Wearable cycle-prediction studies do not all measure the same "temperature" or the same "heart rate". Body site, time of day, aggregation strategy, and accompanying hormone labels all change the problem definition [11] [12] [13].

Signal family Examples in the literature Typical use Main caveat
Temperature BBT, wrist skin temperature, finger skin temperature, distal or peripheral temperature, overnight temperature profiles Ovulation estimation, luteal shift detection, next-menses prediction Signal meaning changes by body site and by whether data are raw, nightly-averaged, or phase-aggregated
Cardiovascular Sleeping HR, resting HR, inter-beat interval, HRV, pulse rate Phase classification, ovulation-day estimation, multimodal fusion Highly sensitive to stress, illness, exercise load, and sleep disruption
Respiratory and autonomic Respiratory rate, electrodermal activity, skin conductance, perfusion-related measures Complementary passive-sensing features in multimodal models Less standardised across devices and less commonly available in open datasets
Sleep and activity context Sleep duration, sleep timing, awakenings, steps, activity counts Context adjustment, feature fusion, quality control Often reflects behaviour as much as physiology
Symptoms and self-report Cramps, discharge, mood, bleeding intensity, medication use Hybrid wearable-plus-diary models Reporting adherence and subjectivity affect label reliability
Reference biomarkers Urinary LH, E3G, PdG, serum estradiol, progesterone, FSH, ultrasound Ground-truth anchoring, subgroup definition, hybrid clinical models Useful for label construction and subgroup definition, but ovulation labelling still requires caution because there is no single universally accepted ground truth across studies

Temperature signals

Temperature is still the most established physiological route because progesterone-linked thermal change gives a plausible post-ovulatory signal. But BBT, wrist temperature, finger temperature, and peripheral skin temperature are not interchangeable [8] [7] [14].

Cardiovascular signals

Sleeping HR and HRV increasingly appear as useful complements to temperature because they track autonomic and circulatory shifts across the cycle. They may help when temperature is noisy, but they also respond strongly to stress, sleep irregularity, and illness [9] [10].

Multimodal passive sensing

Recent work combines temperature, cardiac signals, sleep, activity, and symptoms rather than assuming a single biomarker will solve the task. This is often more realistic, but it also makes feature provenance and missingness harder to interpret [12] [15].

Hormones as labels, not just signals

LH, E3G, PdG, estradiol, progesterone, and FSH matter even when the deployment model is "wearable-only". They often define the labels or validate subgroup physiology, so understanding them is necessary for reading the wearable literature correctly.

Why evaluation is messy

Source of mismatch Example Why it matters
Endpoint mismatch Ovulation day vs fertile window vs next menstruation Reported numbers are not directly comparable
Label mismatch LH onset, LH peak, peak + 1 day, progesterone confirmation, or calendar proxy Two studies may target different operational definitions of the "same" event
Split mismatch Random days, random cycles, leave-last-cycle-out, or subject-wise split Leakage risk changes sharply with the split rule
Cohort mismatch Regular-only, fertility-aware users, or mixed real-world populations Easier cohorts can make methods look stronger than they are
Metric mismatch MAE, within ±2 days, AUC, sensitivity, PPV Different metrics reward different behaviours
  • Subject-wise evaluation is usually the safer choice when the claim is generalisation to unseen people.
  • Window-based accuracy should be paired with a continuous error measure.
  • Papers should explicitly state whether predictions are daily prospective or retrospective full-cycle estimates.

Research gaps that matter now

Irregular populations are still under-analysed

Many studies still compare "regular" and "irregular" users as if irregularity were a single condition. That is too coarse for both interpretation and product design.

Signal taxonomies remain underspecified

Too many papers say "temperature" or "heart rate" without clearly stating body site, aggregation, or whether the values are nocturnal, resting, or continuous.

Open data remain much smaller than commercial datasets

That keeps replication hard and makes subgroup analysis expensive in terms of statistical power.

Uncertainty is still underreported

Real users need calibrated confidence or abstention behaviour, not only a single date prediction.
