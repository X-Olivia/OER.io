Field Survey

Background, taxonomies, methods, performance limits, and research directions.

Background & problem

Menstrual cycle (MC) inference is challenging due to substantial inter- and intra-individual variability, sparse real-world observations, and the indirect nature of physiological proxies. Dysmenorrhoea and related discomforts are highly prevalent and affect daily functioning; care remains largely manual and episodic. From a physiological perspective, the MC is a periodic endocrine-regulated process; ovulation, rather than bleeding, is the key temporal anchor—the luteal phase is relatively stable (e.g. 12–14 days) while the follicular phase varies. In embodied and robotic settings, MC prediction supports proactive decision-making under sparse, multimodal sensing rather than retrospective analysis alone.

Menstrual type taxonomy

Cycle type affects which modelling assumptions apply. Ovulation-based frameworks are not universally applicable: in anovulatory cycles, characteristic progesterone-related patterns are absent.

TypeDescription
Ovulatory cycleFunctional luteal phase; ovulation-related physiological patterns (e.g. post-ovulatory thermal shift, HR/HRV changes) can be used for inference.
Anovulatory cycleNo functional luteal phase; methods relying on ovulation or progesterone shifts are not applicable.
Regular vs irregularCycle length and phase proportions vary within and across individuals; irregular cycles challenge calendar-based and many physiology-based methods.

Research method taxonomy

Computational approaches can be grouped by input (bleeding-only vs physiological signals) and by modelling paradigm (statistical/calendar vs physiology-informed vs data-driven).

CategoryDescription
Calendar-basedUse only self-reported bleeding onset; moving averages, Bayesian cycle-length models, Hidden Semi-Markov Models (HSMM). Scalable and low burden but weak in irregular cycles.
Physiology-based (wearable)Nocturnal/resting signals (WST, HR, HRV, etc.) with ovulation anchored by urinary LH (or similar). Day-level features, then ovulation inference and/or menstrual prediction.
Data-driven MLGradient boosting, RF, SVM, etc. Learn mappings from physiological features to phase or events; performance degrades with irregularity and noise.
Physiology-informed signal processingExploit morphological patterns (e.g. biphasic temperature) to identify ovulation; high accuracy when patterns are clear, abstain when absent.
Probabilistic / density-basedKernel density estimation, logistic regression for fertile window or menstrual onset; calibrated uncertainty, often robust only in regular cohorts.

Overview of existing work

Representative physiological correlates from wearables include: wrist skin temperature (WST) with post-ovulatory elevation; fingertip temperature aligned with ovulation; nocturnal minimum HR (elevated in luteal phase); HF-HRV with peri-ovulatory decline. Ovulation annotation is usually based on urinary LH (surge onset/peak + 1 day, etc.); studies differ in exact definitions. Data-driven and physiology-informed methods both face limits from signal noise, behavioural variability, and annotation uncertainty. Performance is consistently better when predicting menstruation from inferred ovulation than from direct ovulation detection, due to luteal phase stability.

Performance limits

Reported ranges from the literature (regular ovulatory cycles, dense sampling). Sparse or irregular settings typically see larger errors.

Paradigm / metricApprox. range
Data-driven ML (ovulation MAE)≈ 2–4 days; lowest ≈ 2 days in favourable sub-cohorts
Probabilistic (fertile window AUC; menstrual ±2 d)AUC ≈ 0.87; ~83% within ±2 days
Physiology-informed (ovulation MAE; ±2 d accuracy)MAE ≈ 1–2 days in detectable cycles; 78–89% ±2-day
Calendar-based (irregular cycles)Errors can exceed ±8 days

Research hotspots & directions to avoid

Current focus: Ovulation-centred inference under sparse or irregular sampling; multimodal wearable features with hormonally verified labels; uncertainty-aware and robustness to missing data; integration into embodied/robotic systems for proactive support.

Directions to treat with caution or avoid: Purely calendar-based methods for irregular populations without physiological signals; assuming dense daily sampling in long-term deployment; treating ovulation annotation as a single gold standard (operational definitions vary); overfitting complex models on small cohorts without strong regularisation or physiological grounding.
