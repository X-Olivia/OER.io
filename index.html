A research guide to menstrual cycle prediction

This site is designed for new researchers entering menstrual cycle prediction. It explains the field in plain language, separates calendar, wearable, and clinical routes, highlights what is physiologically stable versus variable, and makes the current research bottlenecks explicit.

Why this research matters

The main opportunity in this field is no longer just squeezing a small average gain out of next-date prediction. A more important goal is to use physiology to separate different cycle types and to build models that are more specific to the people whom one-size-fits-all calendar rules serve poorly.

Average accuracy can hide the real problem

Real-world cycle data show substantial variation in cycle length, follicular timing, and ovulation timing, so a single headline score can hide who a model actually works for [1] [2]. In wearable studies, performance typically drops once irregular menstruators are evaluated separately, which is exactly why overall average accuracy is an incomplete target [3] [4].

Different cycle types create different tasks

Short-stable cycles, long cycles, and highly variable cycles are not the same prediction problem. Li et al. showed that users at different ends of the variability spectrum differ in both cycle characteristics and symptom-tracking patterns, which supports subgroup-aware modelling rather than a single pooled model for everyone [2].

Physiology helps break the one-size-fits-all view

Wearable temperature, heart rate, and HRV matter not only because they may improve prediction, but because they expose biological structure that calendar history alone cannot capture. That makes it possible to ask which physiological patterns differ by regularity, age, BMI, or hormonal status, and to build more targeted models from there [5] [3].

Better progress means better stratification

A stronger benchmark is not simply whether average error drops a little. A stronger benchmark is whether the method can characterize under-served groups, explain where it fails, and support personalization for people with different cycle dynamics [2] [4].

The research frontier is shifting from one average score toward physiology-informed, subgroup-aware, and eventually more personalized menstrual modelling.

Start here

1. Learn the biology before the model

The cycle is not a fixed 28-day template. Ovulation is the key temporal anchor, the follicular phase tends to vary more, and the luteal phase is usually more stable. That matters because many prediction errors are really biology errors disguised as modelling errors.

2. Separate the three modelling routes

Calendar methods use prior bleeding history; wearable methods use physiological signals such as skin temperature and heart rate; clinical approaches rely on hormone tests, ultrasound, or more controlled observations. They solve related but not identical problems.

3. Expect data access to be a bottleneck

Public datasets exist, but most large-scale app and device datasets are not openly released. That means evaluation protocols and careful problem definition are as important as model choice.

4. Read results with caution

Reported performance is often hard to compare because studies use different targets, label rules, cohort filters, and metrics. A paper can look strong simply because it evaluated an easier cohort.

The three routes into the field

Route Main inputs Why people use it Main limitation
Calendar / history-based Past bleeding dates, cycle length history, adherence patterns Low burden, scalable, works with app data Breaks down when cycles are irregular or history is sparse
Wearable physiology Skin temperature, heart rate, HRV, sleep, respiration, activity Captures physiological change rather than date history alone Signals are noisy, device-specific, and often weakly labelled
Clinical / biomarker-led Urinary LH, progesterone, ultrasound, blood tests Closer to biological ground truth Higher burden, smaller cohorts, less deployable in daily life

The wearable and clinical routes often interact: many wearable studies still use clinical or hormonal measures to define labels, even when those measures are not available at deployment time.

What makes this area difficult

Menstrual prediction is not just a forecasting problem. It is a sparse-sensing, weak-labelling, person-specific time-series problem with meaningful physiological constraints.

  • Real-world cycles show substantial variation in length, phase timing, and consistency rather than clustering around a single ideal cycle.
  • Ovulation does not reliably occur at the cycle midpoint, so midpoint heuristics are structurally weak.
  • Wearable signals differ by body site and device; wrist skin temperature, finger temperature, and basal temperature should not be treated as interchangeable.
  • Irregular populations are usually collapsed into one coarse category, even though short-stable, long, and high-variability patterns create different prediction problems.
  • Evaluation is inconsistent across studies because labels, splits, and metrics vary widely.

Current research gaps

Irregular cycles are under-served

Many methods are developed or reported on relatively regular cohorts. That makes them look more reliable than they are for the people most likely to need better prediction support.

No shared evaluation standard

Different studies define ovulation differently, report different windows and metrics, and split data at different levels. Comparison across papers is therefore often weak.

Open data remain scarce

Large app and device cohorts are influential but usually proprietary. New researchers are often forced to work with small cohorts, public app exports, or carefully processed subsets.

Deployment still lags behind validation

A model may work retrospectively while failing in a daily prediction setting where only past and present observations are available.
